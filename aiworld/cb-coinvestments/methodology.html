How the numbers are computed

Methodology for the VC capital-flow sankey and the syndication & fragmentation chart · Source: Crunchbase

Both pages are views of the same pre-computed dataset. A single Python script (build_fragmentation.py) classifies every funding round once and writes the aggregates the pages display; no calculation beyond summing and normalising happens in the browser.

1 Data and unit of analysis

The unit of analysis is the funding round. For each round we know the funded company's location, the round type, the amount raised (USD), and the list of participating investors. Each investor is assigned a jurisdiction:

  • US investors → their state (e.g. California, New York);
  • all other investors → their country (e.g. France, Germany).

Investors whose location is unknown are excluded from that round's syndicate. Rounds with no locatable investor at all are dropped.

Company-anchored markets

Rounds are assigned to a market by where the funded company sits, not where the investors sit. A round belongs to the US market if the company is American, and to the EU market if the company is in the selected EU set:

  • EU-27 — the 27 member states;
  • EU+ — EU-27 plus the United Kingdom and EFTA (Switzerland, Norway, Iceland, Liechtenstein).

This makes the two blocs comparable as internal markets: US states play the same role on the US side as member states do on the EU side.

2 Two flags per round

For each round, only investors inside the bloc (US investors for a US round, EU-set investors for an EU round) define the two classifications:

FlagDefinition
Single vs multi-jurisdiction syndicate Count the distinct in-bloc jurisdictions among the round's investors (distinct US states, or distinct member states). Two or more → multi-jurisdiction.
Invests domestically vs cross-border Domestic if at least one investor shares the company's own jurisdiction (same state / same member state); otherwise cross-border.
Edge case. Investors from outside the bloc do not count toward either flag. A round funded exclusively by out-of-bloc investors (e.g. a German company backed only by US funds) therefore has zero in-bloc jurisdictions and is classified single-jurisdiction → cross-border.

3 The sankey

Every round falls into exactly one cell of a 2×2 table: {single, multi} × {domestic, cross-border}. The four cells are the four ribbons of the sankey.

Left nodes: syndicate composition. Right nodes: where the capital goes. Ribbon size = rounds or $ in that cell.
  • Rounds each round counts as 1;
  • $ each round counts its full amount raised (the round total is not split across investors in this view).

Cells are stored per year, so the year picker simply sums the selected years. All percentages on the page are normalisations of the same four cells:

  • node % (above/below each bar) = node total ÷ grand total;
  • flow % at the left end = cell ÷ its source node's total outflow;
  • flow % at the right end = cell ÷ its target node's total inflow.

4 Syndication & fragmentation over time

The line chart plots one value per market per year (2008–2025), toggling between four definitions:

MetricWeightDefinition
SyndicationRounds share of rounds with a multi-jurisdiction syndicate
Syndication$ USD raised in multi-jurisdiction rounds ÷ total USD raised (whole round amounts)
FragmentationRounds share of rounds with no investor from the company's own jurisdiction (1 − domestic share)
Fragmentation$ intra-bloc cross-border capital ÷ total attributed capital (equal-split attribution, below)

Equal-split $ attribution

Round amounts are reported per round, not per investor, so for the dollar-based fragmentation measure each round's USD is split equally across its located investors. Each investor's slice is then labelled:

slice = round USD ÷ number of located investors, labelled
domestic — in-bloc investor, same jurisdiction as the company
intra-bloc cross-border — in-bloc investor, different jurisdiction
international — investor outside the bloc
Fragmentation ($) = intra-bloc cross-border $ ÷ (domestic $ + intra-bloc $ + international $)

Because the split is a partition of each round's amount, summed slices reproduce the raw round totals exactly — the build script verifies this conservation at the end of every run.

5 Interpretation

The two fragmentation definitions deliberately answer different questions. The rounds-based measure asks: did any local investor participate at all? The dollar-based measure asks: what share of the capital crossed an internal border? Read together with syndication, they show how often — and how much — capital is pooled across US states versus across EU member states.
