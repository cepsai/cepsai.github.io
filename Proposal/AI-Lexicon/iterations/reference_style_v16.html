European Union

2 frameworks
Artificial Intelligence Act (2024)
Regulation (EU) 2024/1689
In effect

Puts in place obligations for providers and deployers of AI systems in the EU, drawing on a risk-based classification system that includes prohibited practices (unacceptable risk) and high-risk AI systems. It also regulates AI models, namely general-purpose AI models and general-purpose AI models with systemic risks (over 102510 to the 25th power FLOPs in training compute), with specific obligations in place for this category of providers.

Effective: 1 Aug 2024 Key provisions: Art. 3 · Art. 17 · Art. 52 · Annex III
Code of Practice for General-purpose AI Models (2025)
Under Article 56 AI Act
Voluntary guidance

Formulated under Article 56 of the AI Act, the CoP provides operational guidance for providers of General-Purpose AI (GPAI) models to demonstrate compliance with the obligations listed under the AI Act (Articles 53 and 55). The CoP specifically addresses copyright and transparency, as well as safety and security for GPAI models with systemic risk.

Effective: 10 Jul 2025

California

2 frameworks
SB 53 (2025) — Transparency in Frontier Artificial Intelligence (TFAI)
In effect

Puts in place safety, reporting and risk management obligations for providers of frontier models (over 102610 to the 26th power FLOPs in training compute). Obligations thresholds are put forward according to the revenue of developer, with those having over $500M in annual gross revenues being required to put in place a Frontier AI Framework for risk management. Transparency obligations apply to all developers of frontier models.

Effective: 1 Jan 2026
SB 942 (2024) — California AI Transparency Act
In effect

Requires providers of generative AI systems with over 1M users / visitors to provide AI detection tools and include persistent latent/manifest watermarks in AI-generated content.

Effective: 2 Aug 2026

Colorado

2 frameworks
SB 24-205 (2024) — Colorado AI Act (CAIA)
In effect

Puts in place obligations for developers and deployers of high-risk AI systems with the aim of preventing algorithmic discrimination. These include transparency, impact assessment and risk management obligations for deployers, and documentation requirements for both developers and deployers of high-risk AI systems.

Effective: 30 Jun 2026
SB 25B-004 (2026)
Amendment

Amends the Colorado AI Act, extending the enforcement date from February 2026 to June 2027.

Effective: 30 Jun 2026 Amends: SB 24-205

New York

2 frameworks
A6453B (2025) — Responsible AI Safety and Education Act (RAISE Act)
In effect

Puts in place safety obligations for large developers, that is, developers who used > 102610 to the 26th power FLOPs and > $1M in compute. This bill has been amended by S8828, which will enter into force in January 2027.

Effective: 19 Mar 2026
S8828 (2025)
Amendment

Amends the RAISE Act, aligning it more closely with California SB 53. It introduces frontier developer / large frontier developer thresholds. Large frontier developers are developers who trained a model using > 102610 to the 26th power FLOPs in compute and have > $500M in annual gross revenues. When compared with A6453B, obligations for frontier developers have been expanded through a more comprehensive risk management framework and periodic reporting of catastrophic risk assessment.

Effective: 1 Jan 2027 Amends: A6453B

Texas

1 framework
HB 149 (2025) — Texas Responsible Artificial Intelligence Governance Act (TRAIGA)
In effect

Puts in place obligations for developers and deployers of AI systems centred on technical documentation to prevent algorithmic discrimination. Additional obligations apply to government agencies and healthcare service providers, who must disclose to consumers interaction with AI systems. Developers and deployers who are significantly aligned with national risk management standards are exempt from liability. Liability applies if "intent to discriminate" is verified.

Effective: 1 Jan 2027

Utah

2 frameworks
SB 149 (2024) — Artificial Intelligence Policy Act (AIPA)
In effect

Puts in place obligations for suppliers of generative AI systems centred on transparency. All suppliers of generative AI systems must disclose AI interaction upon individual request, while those operating in high-risk domains (i.e., regulated occupations) must do so at the start of the interaction.

Effective: 1 May 2024
SB 226 (2025)
Amendment

Amends AIPA, limiting disclosure requirements through a narrower definition of generative AI system and requiring disclosure of AI interactions only if the interaction is deemed high-risk (e.g., sensitive data, personalised recommendations), in the context of regulated occupations.

Effective: 7 May 2025 Amends: SB 149 (AIPA)

Methodology

The Digital AI Lexicon follows a six-step research methodology. Each step documents a decision in scoping, selecting, validating and analysing the regulatory corpus.

01 Surveying 02 Selecting 03 Identifying 04 Validating 05 Analysing 06 Incorporating
01

Step 1 · Surveying existing U.S. federal and state-level regulations

Research was conducted to identify relevant developments in the US regulatory landscape with regard to Artificial Intelligence, targeting the period between April 2024 (date of publication of the previous edition of the EU–U.S. Terminology and Taxonomy for Artificial Intelligence) and 28 March 2026. Four findings emerged:

  • The landscape of U.S. federal-level regulations is currently limited, particularly following EO 14179 (2025).
  • Apart from EO 14179, other executive orders have been issued, addressing, for example, "Woke AI" and the "Export of the American AI Technology Stack", among others. However, these have not been deemed relevant to include in the terminology analysis, due to their scope and lack of comparative basis with the EU AI Act.
  • There has been a surge in state-level AI regulations. Several state-level AI regulations have been approved and entered into force, with others at Senate and Assembly levels.
  • Although multiplying at state-level, the AI regulatory landscape in the U.S. might be impacted in the near future by EO 14365 and the U.S. AI Framework.
02

Step 2 · Selecting relevant U.S. federal and state-level regulations

Given the scarcity and limited terminological relevance of federal AI regulations, priority was given to state-level laws. A selection rationale was developed to narrow the number of regulations:

  • Prioritise regulation that has been approved and / or is currently in effect.
  • Prioritise state regulations that have a comprehensive, cross-sectoral AI governance scope, both across and within states.

This step concluded with the selection of nine U.S. state laws, as listed in Primary Sources. These laws span five U.S. states — California, Colorado, New York, Texas and Utah — covering the Republican and Democratic political spectrum.

03

Step 3 · Identifying and selecting relevant terms

The identification of relevant terms proceeded as follows:

  • Identify key terms in the EU AI Act — the baseline for comparative analysis — particularly in Articles 3, 17, 52 and Annex III.
  • Identify key terms in the selected U.S. state regulations.
  • Map and assess high-level overlap of terms identified in U.S. state regulations and the AI Act.
  • Extract terms and thematic clusters from the second edition of the EU–U.S. Terminology and Taxonomy for Artificial Intelligence (2024).

Based on conceptual overlap, regulatory relevance, and novelty relative to the 2024 EU–U.S. Terminology and Taxonomy, 43 concepts were identified, organised into 8 concept groups across 4 thematic clusters.

04

Step 4 · Validating selection of relevant terms

The selection of U.S. state laws and relevant terms for comparative EU–US analysis was presented at a coordination meeting between CEPS and the European Commission. Following the meeting, the European Commission validated the terms deemed necessary for inclusion in the current study.

05

Step 5 · Conducting comparative terminology analysis

The comparative analysis was conducted by collecting, for each term and from the respective regulatory text, the definition, scope and obligations. These elements formed the basis of the analysis and the interpretative notes provided alongside the comparative terminology tables.

06

Step 6 · Incorporating comparative analysis into the Digital AI Lexicon

The terminology tool, named Digital AI Lexicon, was developed alongside the comparative analysis described in Step 05, allowing for a reflexive process to improve both elements of the study.
